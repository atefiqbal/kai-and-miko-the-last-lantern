THE LAST LANTERN

Above the clouds, a boy chases a fox for a stolen ember — and finds a friend trying to bring the light home. A wordless 45-second short about misread intentions and belonging.

45s runtime 1080p · 16:9 3×15s Seedance 12 keyframes storyboard-first

The Story

A theft that wasn't.

Told in three fifteen-second movements, with no dialogue — the picture and the sound carry it.

In a wind-swept village built on rooftops above a sea of cloud, every lantern glows at dusk — every one but a single dark beacon at the edge of the sky. Kai kneels on a maintenance platform with a glowing ember capsule in his bag. Miko, a small fox, watches it a beat too long — then snatches it and runs.

Kai gives chase across slate roofs and a swaying bridge, certain he's been robbed. But at the far tower the fox isn't hiding the ember — he's pawing at a dead mechanism, trying to reach what he cannot fix alone. The boy arrives angry, and then he understands. Not a thief. A keeper of the light.

They work it together: Kai's hands, Miko's paw on a hidden latch, one ember between them. The beacon catches, and warmth spreads tower to tower until the whole village remembers how to glow. Then the two of them sit at the edge of the platform and watch it — home, at last.

Cast

A boy and a fox.

Two characters were given; the world and the story were built around them. Their designs never change across a single frame of the film.

Protagonist · ~10 years

Kai

Energetic, curious, kind-hearted. He carries the ember and the reach — the strength to work the crank and the openness to change his mind. His anger lasts exactly as long as it takes to understand.

Companion · small fox

Miko

Clever, quick, affectionate — and wordless. He sees the failing beacon before anyone else and can't explain it, so he acts. Speed and perception where Kai has reach and strength; neither relights the light alone.

World-building

The Cloud-Top Village.

Before a single shot, two reference sheets locked the place and the prop — so every keyframe and every second of animation inherits the same architecture, materials, and light.

Environment Sheet — Cloud-Top Villageaerial · roof lane · bridge · beacon platform · skyline · route map + materials + palette

A wind-swept alpine settlement of steep slate roofs, half-timbered facades, copper-and-glass lantern towers and suspended timber bridges, floating above dense fog at blue hour. The palette holds cool slate-blue and sage, with amber reserved as a narrative accent — the colour of the ember, the characters, and the light they restore. One beacon stays dark in every shot until the very end.

Object Sheet — The Last Lanternturnaround · mechanism states · ember capsule · crank · hidden latch · power-flow

The beacon is both machine and metaphor: a weathered copper housing with a glass lantern chamber, a hand crank Kai can turn, and a hidden latch beneath that only a small paw can reach. Dark while the characters misunderstand each other; radiant once they cooperate. Its mechanics were designed first so the repair reads clearly on screen — insert the ember, free the latch, turn the crank, and the light returns.

Voiceover & Script

Six lines. The rest is silence.

The film was built wordless, then a spare narrator was added — read by a warm storyteller voice, with the diegetic sound design ducked gently underneath. The chase stays silent on purpose.

  1. Locked the story around a single readable conflict before touching an image: a "theft" that turns out to be a rescue.
  2. Built an environment sheet (6 views) and a beacon object sheet (turnaround + mechanism states) so geography and prop logic were fixed first.
  3. Generated 12 keyframes in GPT Image 2 at 2K, each referencing the four locked sheets; approved one frame per panel against a continuity gate.
  4. Animated three 15-second Seedance 2.0 multishot clips from those keyframes, diegetic sound only, each starting from its approved keyframe.
  5. Edited the three clips, verified the joins, then added a spare narrator voiceover and on-screen script; a HyperFrames composition rendered the captioned master.
More chase → one clear beacon-problem shot. The audience must grasp Miko's intent the instant he reaches the tower, so a still "problem" beat earns its place over extra running.
Bigger finale → a quiet ending. The ignition is the visual climax; the last image releases tension rather than competing with it.
Character sheets as env-sheet refs → text-only sheets. Feeding the character art as image references bled the characters into the environment sheet; describing the style in words kept the sheets character-free.
Wordless first, words few. The film was designed to read with no dialogue; the narrator was added last and kept to six lines so silence still carries the chase.
