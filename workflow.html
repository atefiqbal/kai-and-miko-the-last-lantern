The problem

Your generations are good. Your process is leaking.

You already make strong images and clips. But look at where the hours actually go:

Regenerating shots because a character drifted off-model3–6 hrs
Scrolling chat logs hunting for "that one prompt that worked"1–2 hrs
Re-explaining your world to the model in every new shot1–2 hrs
Posting raw MP4s that bury your craft in a Discord scroll∞
Per project6–10+ hrs of leak

And the worst part isn't the hours. It's that after all that work, your output looks like attempts — not a finished piece with a storyboard, a world, and a page of its own. Here's the thing: both leaks have the same fix.

The one rule

Lock the references first. Pass them into everything.

The whole method in one sentence
Decide WHO (character sheets), WHERE (environment sheet), and WHAT (hero-prop sheet) as locked reference images — before generating a single shot — then attach those same references to every keyframe and every video generation.
That's why nothing drifts across 12 keyframes and three video sequences. The model never gets to reinvent your world, because your world arrives as pinned images in every request. Everything below is just this rule, applied stage by stage.
The pipeline

Seven phases. Each one gates the next.

Nothing advances until it passes a continuity check against the locked references. Slower for an hour, faster for a week.

Prompts are layered, not written.

The part you keep asking about

The HTML layout, dissected.

The film page is one self-contained index.html — inline CSS and JS, zero frameworks, zero CDNs — plus a folder of web-optimized media. Here's its exact anatomy and the rules that make it feel professional.

<nav>sticky, anchor links, blur backdrop
HEROkicker → serif title → logline → fact chips

STORY3 paragraphs + three “movement” cards
CHARACTERS2-up cards: sheet image, bio, color swatches

STORYBOARD12-panel grid from a JS array → lightbox

The ten rules that did the heavy lifting

  1. One file. All CSS/JS inline. It opens from disk, from Pages, from anywhere, forever. No build step to break.
  2. Three type roles. Serif for headlines (human voice), mono for timecodes/labels (metadata), system sans for body (explanation). Instant hierarchy, zero font downloads.
  3. One accent color. The film's amber on a dark ground there; this page flips to clay on ivory. Accent = meaning, not decoration.
  4. Data lives in JS arrays, not markup. The 12 storyboard panels and 7 prompts are arrays; a 10-line loop renders them. Add a panel = add a line.
  5. Version tabs swap one <video> src and preserve the timestamp — four cuts of the film, one player, no page weight.
  6. Thumbs in the grid, full frames in the lightbox. 640px thumbnails with loading="lazy"; click opens the 1600px original. Page stays fast.
  7. Media is compressed for web first. 60MB masters → 16–19MB H.264 with +faststart so streaming starts instantly; images ≤1600px JPEG.
  8. Diagrams are inline SVG. Crisp at any size, styled by the page's own CSS, no image request.
  9. Every prompt block has a copy button. If a reader might reuse text, put a clipboard button on it. That's the difference between showing and sharing.
  10. An exact 1200×630 og.jpg. The link unfurls with a beautiful preview in Discord/X/Slack — half your click-through is this one file.

Replicate it

Five steps to your own version.

  1. Install the skill. Unzip html-effectiveness into ~/.claude/skills/ — Claude now builds single-file pages on request.
  2. Read the prompt pack. Notice the layers: style prefix, identity blocks, panel insert, negatives. Swap in your own characters and world.

  4. Keyframes, then video. 2–4 variations per keyframe, audit, approve one; feed each approved frame to a 15s multishot generation as its start-image.
